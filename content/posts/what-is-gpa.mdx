You filed the ticket eighteen months ago.

You checked the portal in March. Still open. Checked again last month. Still open. You stopped checking. You do the work manually now — every Monday morning, same three portals, same Excel file, same two hours. It's faster than waiting.

The industry calls this a backlog. That's a polite word for it.

---

## What Actually Happened With RPA

Enterprise automation worked. That part is true, and it deserves credit.

The big, structured, IT-visible processes got automated. Payroll transfers. Claims processing at volume. Invoice extraction from standard templates. The CoE delivered real ROI. The case studies are real.

Then something happened. The backlog stopped shrinking.

Automation requests pile up faster than the CoE can deliver. Every process that gets automated surfaces three more requests. Demand scales exponentially. Headcount scales linearly. By the time a request gets scoped, prioritised, resourced, and built — if it survives reprioritisation at all — the business has often moved on or built a manual workaround. The queue isn't a waiting room. It's where requests go to die quietly.

And here's the part nobody talks about openly: a significant chunk of your CoE's capacity isn't building new automation — it's maintaining what's already running. Bots built on screen coordinates break when vendor UIs update. Every quarter, something needs patching. The CoE that was supposed to clear your backlog is busy keeping last year's automations alive.

RPA works brilliantly for a specific type of process: high-volume, stable, rules-based, same every time. For everything outside that — your CoE has a queue. A long one.

---

## The Citizen Developer Was Always a Fantasy

The vendors saw the scaling problem coming. Their answer was citizen development — business users building their own automation with low-code platforms. The promise: democratise automation, clear the backlog, let business users self-serve.

It didn't work. Not because the tools were bad. Because the model was built on a lie.

Real enterprise processes touch four to seven systems. They have exceptions, regulatory dependencies, legacy constraints that change without notice. A business user who learned a drag-and-drop tool last month isn't equipped for that. And when they try, you don't get cleared backlogs — you get hundreds of ungoverned automations running across the enterprise, unknown to IT, with no error handling, no documentation, no owner when the person who built it leaves.

The security team calls it shadow IT. They're right.

## The Last Mile Nobody Reaches

Here is the honest picture.

The CoE reaches the structured, high-volume, IT-visible work — the processes big enough to justify a formal project, stable enough to survive a bot, important enough to make the priority queue. That's not everything. Not even close.

What's left is the last mile: team-level processes, person-specific workflows, tasks that are too small for a formal CoE project but too large to keep doing manually. Think about what that looks like in actual numbers.

A CoE developer — even a junior one at 6 to 8 LPA — cannot justify two to three weeks of work for a process that saves one person two hours a week. The economics don't work for a centralised team with 200 items in the queue. The process is real. The manual work is real. The CoE just wasn't designed to reach it.

Multiply that 2-hour task across a 500-person operations team. You're looking at tens of thousands of hours per year in manual work that the CoE will never prioritise. Not because they don't care. Because they were never designed for the last mile.

That finance analyst running Monday morning reports? In the backlog.
That HR coordinator processing quarterly onboarding documents? In the backlog.
That ops manager compiling a weekly status from five systems? In the backlog.

Still manual. Two years in.

## What GPA Actually Is

Guerrilla Process Automation is business-driven automation built by practitioners, governed by IT, supported by the CoE — without the 18-month wait.

The word guerrilla is intentional. Guerrilla strategy enters where conventional forces can't. Lightweight, adaptive, close to the ground. Not weaker — differently suited to different terrain.

The CoE is the conventional force. It operates at scale, handles the high-volume structured work, and does it well. GPA is guerrilla — last-mile, team-level, practitioner-owned. Terrain the CoE was never designed for. The two aren't competing. They're covering different ground in the same organisation.

Three things make it different from the approaches that failed:

**Business-driven.** The person who does the work builds the automation. They know the process better than any developer who'll spend three hours in a requirements meeting trying to understand it.

**IT-governed.** Not shadow IT. GPA runs on IT-approved infrastructure. Same procurement ticket, same approved browser, same managed laptop. The difference is who builds it, not where it runs.

**CoE-supported.** The CoE doesn't lose relevance — it upgrades. From building everything to governing everything. More coverage, same team.

---

## The People In It

**Guerrilla Practitioner** — the business user who owns their automation. Records, runs, and manages processes within CoE-approved guardrails. No coding required. Their Monday morning changes.

**Guerrilla Bot Developer** — the technical backbone. Builds the reusable components, handles complex UI automation logic, and develops the workflow and Excel intelligence that makes practitioner-owned automation actually work at enterprise quality. This is product engineering — not bot scripting.

**Guerrilla Architect** — the CoE bridge. Governs the GPA practice inside an organisation, certifies practitioners, owns the framework that connects last-mile automation to enterprise IT. The person who finally gives the CoE a scaling answer that isn't "hire more developers."

---

## Where It Fits

GPA isn't a replacement for RPA or the CoE. It's the extension that finally covers what they were never designed to reach.

The processes sitting in your queue today — the ones that have been there for a year, two years, that your team has stopped asking about — those are the last mile. That's what GPA is for.

> The automation gap isn't a technology problem. It's a distribution problem.
