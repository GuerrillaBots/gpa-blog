The industry calls this a backlog. That's a polite word for it.

## What Actually Happened With RPA

Enterprise automation worked. That part is true, and it deserves credit.

The big, structured, IT-visible processes got automated. Payroll transfers. Claims processing at volume. Invoice extraction from standard templates. The CoE delivered real ROI. The case studies are real.

Then something happened. The backlog stopped shrinking.

Here's the math: the average enterprise CoE has an 18 to 24-month delivery backlog. Every process that gets automated surfaces three more requests. Demand scales exponentially. Headcount scales linearly. The gap grows permanently.

And here's the other math nobody puts in the case studies: 70 to 75% of your RPA spend goes to maintenance and firefighting — not new automation. Your bots run on screen coordinates, not logic. Every time a vendor updates a UI — which happens quarterly — bots break. Your CoE isn't building anymore. It's patching.

RPA works brilliantly for the top 5 to 10% of processes: high-volume, stable, rules-based, same every time. For everything else, you're fighting the tool.

---

## The Citizen Developer Was Always a Fantasy

The vendors saw the scaling problem coming. Their answer was citizen development — business users building their own automation. Low-code platforms. Drag-and-drop interfaces. The promise: your business users become automation builders.

It didn't work. Not because the tools were bad. Because the model was built on a lie.

Real enterprise processes touch four to seven systems. They have exceptions. Regulatory dependencies. Legacy constraints that change without notice. A business user building on a platform they learned last Tuesday isn't equipped for that. And when they try, you get something worse than a backlog: hundreds of ungoverned automations running across the enterprise, unknown to IT, with no error handling, no documentation, no owner when the person who built it leaves.

The security and governance teams called it shadow IT. They were right.

## The 40% Nobody Reached

Here is the honest picture of enterprise automation.

The CoE can reach structured, high-volume, stable processes. Let's call that 60% of the automation opportunity. What's left — the last 40% — lives at the team level, the process level, the individual level. Small tasks. Person-specific workflows. Things too variable for a formal bot, too important to leave manual.

A CoE developer costs ₹30 to 40 lakhs per year all-in. They cannot justify three weeks building automation for a 2-hour weekly task that affects one person. The economics don't work. But that 2-hour task is still 100 hours of manual work per year that didn't need to happen.

Multiply across a 500-person operations team. That's 50,000 hours per year of inefficiency the CoE will never reach. Not because they don't care. Because they were never designed for the last mile.

That finance analyst filing Monday reports? In the backlog.
That HR coordinator processing onboarding documents every quarter? In the backlog.
That ops manager compiling a weekly status report from five systems? In the backlog.

They're still doing it manually. Two years in.

## What Guerrilla Process Automation Actually Is

Guerrilla Process Automation is business-driven automation built by practitioners, governed by IT, supported by the CoE — without the 18-month wait.

The word guerrilla is intentional. Guerrilla strategy enters where conventional forces can't. Lightweight, adaptive, close to the ground. Not because it's weaker — because the terrain demands it.

The CoE is the conventional force. It handles structured, high-volume, standardized processes. It operates at scale. GPA is guerrilla — team-level, person-level, last-mile. The terrain the CoE was never designed for. The two fight the same war.

Three things separate GPA from the approaches that failed:

**Business-driven.** The person who does the work builds the automation. They already know the process better than any developer who'll spend three hours in a requirements meeting trying to understand it. Give them the right tool and they'll automate it faster, more accurately, and with more ownership than a CoE ticket ever produced.

**IT-governed.** Not shadow IT. Not an Excel macro on a server nobody approved. GPA runs on IT-approved infrastructure — the same procurement ticket, the same approved browser, the same laptop your company already manages. The difference is who builds it, not where it runs.

**CoE-supported.** The CoE doesn't lose work — it upgrades. Instead of building every automation themselves, CoE developers build the frameworks and guardrails that business users build *within*. They review what goes live. They scale what works. They become the force multiplier, not the bottleneck.

---

## The Three Roles

**Guerrilla Practitioner** — the business user. Records processes, owns automations, builds within CoE-approved guardrails. No coding. No waiting. Their Monday morning changes.

**Guerrilla Developer** — the technical builder. Doesn't build bots. Builds the tools, frameworks, and components that practitioners build inside. It's harder than building bots. Significantly more impactful. You're building leverage, not scripts.

**Guerrilla Architect** — the CoE extension. Governs the GPA practice, certifies practitioners, owns the framework that connects the last mile to enterprise IT. The person who finally gives the CoE a backlog solution that isn't "hire more developers."

## Where This Fits

RPA is not going anywhere. Neither is the CoE. GPA isn't a replacement — it's the extension that makes both actually work.

The CoE handles the 60%: structured, high-volume, IT-native. GPA handles the 40%: last-mile, team-level, practitioner-owned. Together, you have complete automation coverage for the first time.

The 40% has been sitting in your ticket queue for two years. It's still manual. Your people are still doing it every Monday morning.

That's what this is for.
