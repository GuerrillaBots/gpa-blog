Enterprise automation promised to free up workers from repetitive work. Twelve years later, most of the repetitive work is still happening. The people doing it are still doing it. The bots that were supposed to replace it are somewhere in a CoE backlog, waiting for a developer who has 47 other priorities.

This is not a technology failure. It's a distribution failure. The automation got built — just not for the right people.

Guerrilla Process Automation (GPA) is the fix.

---

## What GPA Is

Guerrilla Process Automation is business-driven automation built by practitioners, governed by IT, and supported by the CoE — without the 18-month wait.

Three parts to that definition.

**Business-driven.** The automation is designed by the person who does the work — the billing analyst who reconciles invoices every Monday, the HR coordinator who processes 40 onboarding documents per quarter, the ops manager who compiles the weekly status report from five different systems. They know exactly what needs automating. They've known for years. GPA gives them the tool to do it.

**Governed by IT.** GPA does not mean shadow IT. It does not mean running an unsanctioned macro on a server nobody knows about. GPA runs on IT-approved infrastructure — user laptops, approved browsers, tools that cleared the same procurement ticket as any other software. The difference is that the user builds the automation, not IT.

**Supported by the CoE.** The Intelligent Automation Center of Excellence doesn't disappear in a GPA world — it upgrades. Instead of building every automation themselves, CoE developers build the frameworks and guardrails that business users build *within*. They review and govern what goes to production. They scale what's working. They become the force multiplier, not the bottleneck.

## The Last Mile Problem

In logistics, "the last mile" is the final leg of delivery — from the distribution center to the customer's door. It's the most expensive, most failure-prone part of the supply chain. It's where scale economies break down and individual variation takes over.

Automation has a last mile too.

The CoE is the distribution center. It handles high-volume, structured, standardized deliveries. But the last mile — the 40% of automation opportunity that lives at the process level, at the team level, at the individual level — is where the CoE's economies of scale break down.

A CoE developer cannot spend 3 weeks building automation for a 2-hour weekly task that affects one person. The economics don't work. But that 2-hour task is still 100 hours per year of manual work that didn't need to happen.

Multiply that across a 500-person operations team. Multiply across 10 teams. You're looking at tens of thousands of hours per year in inefficiency that the CoE will never reach.

GPA reaches it.

---

## Why "Guerrilla"

Guerrilla strategy enters where conventional forces can't — lightweight, adaptive, close to the ground. Not because it's weaker. Because the terrain demands it.

The RPA CoE is conventional. Structured, governed, optimized for scale. GPA is guerrilla — team-level, process-level, person-level. It operates in terrain the CoE was never designed for.

The two aren't in conflict. Guerrilla forces and conventional forces fight the same war. GPA and the CoE serve the same organization.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A finance analyst has a Monday morning routine. She opens three portals, copies data, pastes it into Excel, runs lookups, generates a report, emails it to four people. Every Monday. Two hours.

She filed a ticket in January. Got a reference number. Checked the portal in June. Still open.

She accepted it. Manual work, two hours, every Monday. For two years.

With GPA, she records that process once. The bot runs it while she makes coffee. She doesn't become a developer. She becomes someone who builds automation that works for her.

That's a Guerrilla Practitioner. The people RPA forgot. The 40% nobody talks about.

---

## The Three Roles in a GPA World

**Guerrilla Practitioner** — the business user. Records processes, owns automations, builds within CoE-approved guardrails. No coding required.

**Guerrilla Developer** — the technical builder. Builds the tools, frameworks, and components that Practitioners build inside. Harder than building bots. More impactful.

**Guerrilla Architect** — the CoE extension. Governs the GPA practice, certifies Practitioners, owns the framework that connects GPA to enterprise IT. Bridges the last mile to the CoE.

---

## Where This Fits

GPA is not a replacement for RPA. It's not shadow IT. It's not citizen development rebranded.

It's the practice your automation strategy was missing.

The CoE handles the 60% — the big, structured, IT-native processes. GPA handles the 40% — the last mile. Together, you have complete automation coverage.

That's the goal.

> The automation gap isn't a technology problem. It's a distribution problem. GPA is the distribution fix.
