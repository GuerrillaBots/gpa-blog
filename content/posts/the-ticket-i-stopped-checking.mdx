The ticket is still open. The reconciliation has been running for six weeks. I haven't closed it — closing it feels like mourning something nobody showed up for.

The request was not complicated. Monthly vendor reconciliation: three portals, one Excel template, first week of every month. Our ERP, the GSTN portal, a vendor payment tracker. Four and a half hours if nothing breaks. Five or six if something does. I had been doing this exact thing for three years by the time I filed the ticket.

The CoE acknowledged receipt. They said it was in the pipeline.

Fourteen months later, the status still said "In Review." I stopped checking around month eight. Not dramatically — there was no moment where I slammed anything shut. I just stopped opening the portal because every time I did, nothing had moved, and I would feel something between amusement and low-grade despair that doesn't have a clean name.

My ticket sits below a supply chain build involving SAP, a payments gateway, and fourteen approval nodes. I understand prioritization. Our CoE is competent and badly understaffed. That does not make Thursday shorter.

---

What I eventually understood is that the ticket was never going to clear. Not because the CoE forgot it. Because the CoE was built for work that's larger than mine — processes complex enough to need a developer, stable enough to maintain, large enough to survive an ROI filter. My reconciliation is real work. It's just not that kind of work.

I heard about Guerrilla Process Automation through someone in our shared finance ops channel. She had recorded a payables workflow in her own browser using something called I² Recorder, and she was showing the team like she'd done something she shouldn't have. She hadn't. IT had approved the infrastructure. The CoE supported the setup. The idea behind GPA is that the person who knows the workflow does the recording — because they are the only one who actually knows the workflow.

I was skeptical for about a week.

Then I had a particularly long Thursday — the GSTN portal was running slow, one vendor had quietly changed their reference number format mid-month — and I finished at 6:40 in the evening with a stiff neck and a very cold coffee. The following Monday I recorded the workflow. Three sessions, one per portal. Start to finish, fifty minutes. Most of that was my own walking pace, not the tool. I² Recorder is not magic. You still have to know what you are doing. But if you know what you are doing — and I did, I had been doing this exact reconciliation for three years — the recording is almost mechanical in the best way. You just do the work and let it watch you.

---

Last Thursday the bot ran while I was in my 10 AM standup. By the time the call ended and I opened my laptop, the Excel was populated. I spent twenty minutes reviewing it — I always will, I am not sending month-end numbers to anyone without reading them myself. Then I made chai. I looked at some work I had been pushing to next week. I left at 5:30.

I knew exactly how to do this workflow. I had known it for three years.

Someone just built a tool where that was enough.
