The more bots we build, the longer the backlog grows. That is not a bug. It is what success looks like at scale, and most CoE leads I know have stopped pretending otherwise.

I've been in intelligent automation for sixteen years. 380 bots live in production. Six clients. We make real ROI. I also have 240 items in backlog and I've finally accepted that 190 of them will never get built. That is not a complaint. It is a structural observation, and I think it matters for where intelligent automation goes next.

---

When I built this CoE, I did everything right — at least by the standards we had. Governance framework. ROI filter with a minimum threshold. Quarterly prioritization sessions with business heads. Intake forms, effort estimation templates, a review board. The process was clean. The backlog still grew, because demand for automation compounds. Every time we delivered a bot that saved twenty hours a week, three people on the same floor saw it happen and submitted new requests. The pipeline was a function of our own success, and we had no mechanism to make it self-clearing.

I hired more developers. The backlog grew faster than I hired.

What I eventually understood — and this took longer than I'll admit — is that the CoE model was not designed for this problem. It was designed for a different problem entirely. Enterprise RPA was built for high-volume, IT-visible, CFO-measurable processes: invoice processing at scale, bank reconciliation across 400 accounts, claims adjudication with an audit trail. Processes that are complex enough to need developer expertise, stable enough to maintain, and large enough to survive a proper ROI filter.

That is work my team is genuinely good at.

The 240 items in my backlog are not that work. They are real problems — a relationship manager who exports the same portal report every Monday and pastes it into three sheets, an operations analyst who checks six vendor sites to flag delivery mismatches, a billing executive whose entire monthly close involves forty minutes of copy-paste across tabs. Too small, too person-specific, or too low-frequency to clear my ROI filter. They sit in backlog not because they are unimportant to the person waiting. Because the structure I built was never meant to handle them.

---

This is where I think Guerrilla Process Automation is doing something genuinely useful. Business users record their own workflows in a Chrome extension. IT approves the infrastructure once. The CoE supports but doesn't have to build. It's a second pipe — narrower, appropriate for narrower work. The things that will never survive my ROI filter are exactly the things a trained business user can record, validate, and run on their own machine, under IT-approved tooling, without filing a ticket and waiting six months.

I don't have to choose between governance and coverage anymore.

---

I want to make one more point, because I hear a lot of talk about agentic AI in CoE circles right now and I think we are skipping a step.

The pitch is: AI agents handle exceptions autonomously, and where they cannot, they escalate to a human-in-the-loop. I understand the vision. I've been in enough vendor briefings to have it memorised. But here is the thing — before you can have a human-in-the-loop for an AI agent, you need a human who has time to be in the loop. Right now, in every BPO floor I have visited, the humans who would theoretically be in that loop are the same people doing forty minutes of copy-paste at month-close. You cannot ask someone to supervise an AI agent at 11 AM if they are still manually reconciling a bank statement from the previous Friday.

GPA is not a detour from the agentic future. It is the prerequisite.

Free the practitioners first. Then give them agents to supervise. The sequence matters — and almost nobody in this industry is talking about it.

---

The CoE has a ceiling. Most people who run CoEs have known it for years. We just didn't have a clean way to say it, and we didn't have a solution that didn't require admitting the model had limits.

We do now.
